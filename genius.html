Parking Genius

Mobile application for IoT based parking solution.

To comply with my non-disclosure agreement, I have changed and obfuscated confidential information in this case study. The information in this case study is my own and does not necessarily reflect the views of Fybr.

Parking has become a problem for communities around the world. According to a report, more than 25% of drivers kill more than 15 minutes each day looking for a parking spot and over 80% say their problem is not knowing if there are free spots available or not.

The aim of this project was to design a solution which could help the drivers navigate through the city's complex parking environment and provide them with free and affordable parking spots.

Senior User Experience Designer | Fybr

Market Study

I did a brief market study that included going through the apps, their functionalities, and the data they capture. The primary research was a good starting point for developing insights and being aware of the market. This helped in improving the solution and addressing the latent need. The research was synthesised in a competitor analysis sheet to better understand the market I was about to enter.

Insights market study

The key insights drawn from this study were -

Parking space availability is predicted based on historical trends, instead of real-time data.
Overwhelming amount of information. This results in the screen getting cluttered with placards. Expecting the user to take an informed decision in this high pressure leads to driver stress.
The indexed results for a query aren’t always best for the user. For example, the parking availability may be the highest in a residential neighbourhood which might be 2 km away from the user’s destination.

User Interview

This product is targeted towards the North American market, and being a person who has never traveled to that part of the world, I needed some micro and macro-level understanding of the subject. Aside from understanding the users, I also needed to understand the parking system and pain points which arise from a user perspective.

Talking to the users offered confirmation on many data points that were derived during the course of the primary research.

I interviewed six drivers who helped me understand the subject as well as share their own experiences, challenges, pain-points, and needs. I created a rough guideline of open-ended questions related to the topic and conducted 1-to-1 interviews.

Interview Questions and Insights

Insights from user interviews

The local street rules and signs are extremely confusing. No standardisation existed.
Constant search for free or affordable parking options as close to their destination as possible. They use Google maps to find parking spots and estimated walking distance.
Users were at times compelled to park further away from their destination because they were unaware of nearby parking spaces.
The time left on the parking meter is often calculated by the user. They use mobile phone alarms for this.

Design Highlights

How do you reserve previously booked parking spots?

The feedback we received from some of our users was their was no place they could go to reserve or book the parking spot they liked before. This made us reconsider our home screen. Earlier we used to take the user directly to the map.

We decided to first ask the user “where would they like to park?”. Near their current location, near a specific destination, or in places they have previously parked.

Is it a traffic map?

In our earlier iterations we used we used green to represent an available spot, red an occupied spot, and grey for a closed or no parking. We did not address disabled spots, meter spots and time-limit spots.

When some users saw the map for the first time, they instantly thought that the map was representing traffic in the city but not parking. While others got overwhelmed with the amount of information we were giving. The split up spots added to their confusion.

We decided to never use green-red colour palette together and came up with this alternative colours for different types parking. Green for open, Dark blue for meter and light blue for garages and lots. The disabled spots were marked orange to draw attention to them. We also used iconography along with the colours to handle cases with colour-blindness.

How many parking spots to display without cluttering the screen?

Earlier we used to display all the parking data that was available with us. This led the map to be over crowded and cluttered. It was one of the problems that were mentioned in our user research as well.

We came up with the concept of a “parking score”. This was calculated based upon the availability of a parking spot + distance from the destination + price + historical data of occupancy. This helped us filter out the spots that might be relevant to the user. The user could see a maximum of 10 spots on the screen. If the user wished to view more spots around a location, they could zoom and view them.

Are we page trashing?

Previously, when the user tapped on a street or a placard we directed them to the complete details page. This was a point of frustration for our users.

The solution we created was to give basic information to the user when they tap on the card. If the user is interested in the details of that spot, they can simply slide to view these details. This interaction being a common one seemed as the best solution.

Street rules are confusing.

One of the recurring insights we received during our user research was that the street rules were confusing for many users.

We tried to address this by doing a small data visualisation exercise on our detailed information view to show when a particular street or garage is closed or free of charge or paid.

How much time can one park for?

The data visualisation exercise provided a brief context to the users about the parking hours. But, it didn’t really solve the problem. The users still had to calculate the time remaining on their parking meter.

We decided to send them notification 1 hour before the time was about to expire. If the notifications were turned off we sent them an email and a SMS alert.

How does one look for only open streets?

Over a period of time we received requests from our users that they would like to filter the data available on the map. For example: Viewing only open garages, finding disability parking spots etc.

This made us decide to introduce the filter feature. The data points were gathered over-time from our user feedback.

Key Learnings and Takeaways

This was a really exciting and intensively researched project for me to work on. However, shifting priorities, changing requirements and reprioritization caused a lot of confusion and the scope of the project kept changing. I had to adapt to these changes and deliver the product within the given timelines.

Designing for a different location and culture is fiendishly difficult. At first, I assumed it would simply require me to translate British english to American English, using dollar instead of rupee; but is far more complex than that. I had to learn and design solutions that were colour blind friendly. Use the colour psychology that worked with the American audiences. Learn a completely different system of parking.
